# Gameplay Fusion Patterns

Use these patterns when a visual novel needs gameplay beyond ordinary menus. Choose one primary pattern and one supporting pattern. Too many systems dilute the writing.

## Fusion Test

A mechanic belongs in the VN only if it passes at least three tests:

- It creates scenes the prose alone would not create.
- It changes what the player notices or fears.
- It gives characters new ways to pressure each other.
- It produces consequences that can be paid off in dialogue or route access.
- It expresses the work's central emotion through player behavior.
- Removing it would damage the story structure.

## Pattern 1: Trait-Gated Roleplay

Reference shape: Scarlet Hollow, RPG dialogue tags, ChoiceScript stats.

Player chooses traits or builds at the start. Traits unlock dialogue, perceptions, solutions, blind spots, and relationship changes.

Use when:

- The protagonist is semi-self-insert.
- Replayability should reveal different facts.
- The player should feel authored by their own build.

Variables:

- Trait flags.
- Relationship axes.
- Knowledge tags.
- One or two passive consequence flags per trait.

Failure mode:

- One trait becomes the obvious best trait.
- Trait options become only extra jokes.
- Trait gates replace scene design instead of enriching it.

Countermeasures:

- Give every trait unique information and unique trouble.
- Let a trait solve one crisis while worsening another.
- Write NPC preferences for traits, not universal approval.

## Pattern 2: Relationship Axes

Reference shape: Scarlet Hollow's multi-axis dynamics, dating sims, trust/fear/debt systems.

Track more than affection. Use axes such as trust, fear, debt, attraction, respect, suspicion, intimacy, and ideological alignment.

Use when:

- Characters should react to the way the player treats them.
- Romance or alliance is complicated by fear, debt, or politics.
- Routes depend on social texture.

Failure mode:

- All axes collapse into approval.
- The player optimizes numbers instead of inhabiting scenes.

Countermeasures:

- Hide numbers unless the genre expects visible stats.
- Make high values carry costs.
- Let some scenes require conflicting axes.

## Pattern 3: Evidence And Contradiction

Reference shape: evidence-confrontation and investigation VN structure.

The player collects facts, testimony, objects, alibis, recordings, memories, or contradictions, then uses them in confrontations.

Use when:

- The core fantasy is reasoning under pressure.
- Dialogue should become combat.
- The player should earn accusations.

Variables:

- Evidence inventory.
- Witness reliability.
- Contradiction flags.
- Suspicion or patience meter.

Failure mode:

- Pixel-hunting or guess-the-author logic.
- Evidence becomes a key item with no interpretive work.

Countermeasures:

- Let wrong evidence produce characterful failure lines.
- Give each evidence item a short "why it matters" note.
- Require the player to connect motive, means, timing, and lie.

## Pattern 4: Perception Loop

Reference shape: Slay the Princess, time-loop VNs, psychological horror.

Player choices alter how the world or key character appears in the next loop. Previous decisions become voices, biases, forms, or locked instincts.

Use when:

- The story is about perception, memory, guilt, obsession, or repeated harm.
- Replay is central.
- Each route should feel like a theory of the same person.

Variables:

- Previous ending.
- Dominant action type.
- Trust/violence/avoidance flags.
- Voice or bias unlocked.

Failure mode:

- Loops feel like reset padding.
- The player must repeat too much unchanged content.

Countermeasures:

- Change the first screen after every loop.
- Give each loop a new reading lens.
- Collapse repeated setup into sharper, stranger beats.

## Pattern 5: Parallel POV Timeline

Reference shape: parallel-protagonist and cross-route consequence structure.

The player switches protagonists or perspectives. Actions in one route alter another route's available scenes or endings.

Use when:

- The story is about systems, misunderstandings, or intersecting lives.
- The player should solve the story as a timeline puzzle.
- Bad ends are clues.

Variables:

- Timeline event state.
- Character decision flags.
- Cross-route locks.
- Ending collection.

Failure mode:

- Complexity becomes invisible.
- Players cannot tell what changed.

Countermeasures:

- Provide a map, event log, or timeline.
- Keep each node concise.
- Name the causal link when a route unlocks.

## Pattern 6: Storylet Exploration

Reference shape: Fallen London, 80 Days, Roadwarden, storylet-based IF.

Scenes unlock based on conditions, resources, locations, time, relationships, and previous facts. The player assembles their path from available pieces.

Use when:

- The world should feel broad and explorable.
- Quest order matters.
- The player should pursue leads, favors, routes, or rumors.

Variables:

- Location.
- Time/phase.
- Resource states.
- Progress qualities.
- Menace or pressure.

Failure mode:

- The plot loses momentum.
- The player sees disconnected vignettes.

Countermeasures:

- Maintain a main spine.
- Use recurring NPCs and unresolved objects.
- Let optional scenes feed into a visible investigation board or relationship web.

## Pattern 7: Calendar / Training / Stat Pressure

Reference shape: Long Live the Queen, Princess Maker descendants, school VNs.

The player spends limited time to train skills, manage mood, prepare for events, and pass checks.

Use when:

- The story is about preparation, growth, governance, school life, survival, or role identity.
- Failure should arise from previous priorities.
- The player should sacrifice one competence to gain another.

Variables:

- Time units.
- Mood modifiers.
- Skills.
- Upcoming event thresholds.
- Injury/stress/fatigue.

Failure mode:

- Spreadsheet play detaches from character drama.
- Players need a walkthrough to survive.

Countermeasures:

- Telegraph upcoming domains through scenes.
- Make partial success interesting.
- Tie skill growth to relationships and self-image.

## Pattern 8: Resource Scarcity / Dice / Risk

Reference shape: Citizen Sleeper, Roadwarden, tabletop-inspired narrative RPGs.

The player uses limited dice, health, money, food, reputation, stress, or favors to pursue scenes. Low resources create new story states.

Use when:

- The theme involves precarity, debt, illness, exile, hunger, or survival.
- The player should plan routes and accept imperfect outcomes.

Variables:

- Action currency.
- Risk level.
- Clocks.
- Injury/stress.
- NPC debt.

Failure mode:

- Randomness erases authored emotion.
- Players hoard resources and avoid drama.

Countermeasures:

- Turn low rolls into complications, not dead air.
- Reward risky roleplay with unique information.
- Make resource recovery socially costly.

## Pattern 9: Translation / Interpretation

Reference shape: Heaven's Vault, linguistic puzzles, symbolic mystery.

The player interprets text, symbols, dreams, memories, body language, or artifacts. Their interpretation changes narration and future hypotheses.

Use when:

- The story is about history, grief, unreliable records, archaeology, code, religion, prophecy, or alien contact.
- The player should feel clever through reading carefully.

Variables:

- Glossary/dictionary.
- Hypothesis tags.
- Artifact context.
- Confidence level.
- Misinterpretation flags.

Failure mode:

- Puzzle becomes detached from character stakes.
- Wrong guesses only block progress.

Countermeasures:

- Let interpretations color dialogue.
- Make uncertainty narratively productive.
- Allow correction through later evidence.

## Pattern 10: Narrative Roguelite Continuity

Reference shape: Hades-style remembered repetition, loop routes.

Mechanical failure or repetition advances relationships and story. NPCs remember attempts, jokes, grudges, injuries, and previous outcomes.

Use when:

- Repetition is part of the genre or premise.
- The protagonist can return after failure.
- The story wants gradual intimacy through repeated attempts.

Variables:

- Attempt count.
- Last failure source.
- NPC memory flags.
- Unlock tiers.
- Run-specific discoveries.

Failure mode:

- The loop turns into grind.
- Dialogue repeats too often.

Countermeasures:

- Prioritize recent, rare, and high-salience callbacks.
- Let small "pointless" interactions create affection.
- Change NPC posture after repeated failure.

## Building A Hybrid

Use this process:

1. Name the central player behavior: accuse, confess, hide, train, translate, survive, loop, investigate, seduce, govern.
2. Pick the mechanic that makes that behavior repeat under changing pressure.
3. Define three variables only. Add more after the first playable slice works.
4. Design one scene where the mechanic changes a line, one where it changes a route, and one where it changes an ending.
5. Add a fail state that reveals story value.
6. Playtest whether the player can state what the system is listening for.
