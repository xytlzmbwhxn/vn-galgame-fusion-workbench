# VN Fusion Writer

## Overview

Use this skill to design and write visual novels where prose, dialogue, choice, variables, UI rhythm, and gameplay mechanics reinforce the same dramatic pressure. Treat writing quality and system design as one problem.

## Workflow

1. Define the playable promise in one sentence: the player role, the pressure, the kind of decision the work will repeatedly ask for, and the emotional cost of playing well.
2. Pick a structure: kinetic, common route plus character routes, branch-and-bottleneck, storylet pool, case board, parallel POV, time loop, stat calendar, or hub-and-spoke investigation.
3. Build the cast as conflict engines. For each major character, define desire, fear, social mask, private language, forbidden topic, and what they do when cornered.
4. Choose mechanics by theme. A mechanic must create scenes, reveal character, alter available knowledge, change relationships, spend resources, or reorder routes. Decorative minigames are rejected.
5. Draft a route map before prose: spine scenes, optional scenes, lock points, flags, relationship axes, failure states, ending logic, and replay incentives.
6. Write scene cards before full script: location, visual state, BGM mood, entering pressure, spoken agenda, private agenda, turn, choice, variable effects, exit image.
7. Draft in VN rhythm: short textbox beats, visible staging, sprite/emotion changes, silence as a beat, choices placed at pressure peaks rather than at exposition pauses.
8. Run the style QA pass. Remove generic AI habits, thesis paragraphs, symmetrical contrast formulas, explainy emotional labels, and choices with an obvious optimal answer.

## Scene Contract

Every important scene must answer these questions:

- What changes by the end that cannot be undone?
- What does each speaker want from the conversation right now?
- What is each speaker refusing to say directly?
- What does the player learn, spend, risk, or misread?
- Which later line, choice, or route will remember this scene?

Dialogue lines should usually do at least two jobs: characterize, pressure, misdirect, expose, seduce, threaten, stall, test, confess, or alter state.

## Choice Contract

Use choices to reveal or change one of these:

- Identity: who the player character becomes under pressure.
- Knowledge: what is seen, hidden, misunderstood, or proven.
- Relationship: trust, fear, debt, attraction, resentment, leverage.
- Resource: time, money, health, suspicion, composure, evidence, favors.
- Route access: flags, locks, side scenes, perspective shifts, bad ends, true route keys.
- World state: an NPC leaves, a rumor spreads, an object moves, a deadline advances.

Avoid choice menus where one option is clearly virtuous, optimal, or complete while the others feel like traps. Balance options by mixing gains and costs.

## Hard Style Stops

When drafting creative text, do not use formulaic explanation or balanced contrast as a default move. Especially avoid patterns equivalent to:

- "not X, but Y"
- "rather than X, Y"
- "the real X is Y"
- "this is not only X, it is also Y"
- "in some sense"
- "they realized"
- stock frozen-air imagery
- stock fate/gear imagery
- paragraph endings that summarize the theme for the reader

Replace these habits with action, setting pressure, concrete objects, broken rhythm, unfinished lines, and character-specific speech.

## Default Deliverable Shape

For a new gameplay-fused VN, produce:

1. Concept: title, logline, player role, dramatic promise.
2. Core loop: read, inspect, choose, spend, branch, return, remember.
3. Cast: voice cards and relationship tensions.
4. Mechanics: variables, clocks, route locks, evidence/trait/stat systems.
5. Route map: common route, major branches, bottlenecks, endings.
6. Chapter outline: scene cards with state changes.
7. Script sample: playable VN-style scene with menu choices and variable notes.
8. QA notes: style risks, branch risks, production risks.
